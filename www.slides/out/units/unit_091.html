Representation

We've been making use of a notion of representation.
For example, when discussing this study I said it provides evidence that infants represent objects they aren't seeing.
Before going too much further it would be good to try to say something about this notion.
First, why do we need representation?
I think there's a simple reason. In this study the infants' behaviours are affected by an object which they aren't seeing.
For this to happen, the object must somehow make its mark.
It must bruise the infants, for example.
But since there was no physical contact, the mark can only be psychological.
To a first approximation, 'representation' is a term for the what happens when an object leaves its mark on you psychologically.

source: Baillargeon et al (1985, figure 1)

We can improve on this first approximation by invoking a theory of representation ...
source: \citep[p.\ 84, figure 4.1]{Dretske:1988sq}

(source: Dretske 1988, p. 84 figure 4.1)

‘Once C is recruited as a cause of M---and recruited as a cause of M because of what it indicates about F---C acquires, thereby, the function of indicating F. Hence, C comes to represent F. C acquires its semantics, a genuine meaning, at the very moment when a component of its natural meaning (the fact that it indicates F) acquires an explanatory relevance.’

\citep[p.\ 84]{Dretske:1988sq}

(Dretske 1988, p.\ 84)

To say that infants represent objects in something Dretske's sense of representation is not to say that they have concepts of objects,nor that they have anything like what Fodor calls a languaue of thought.
